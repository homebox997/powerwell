Heart Disease in Senior Dogs: Complete Guide 2026

Heart disease affects up to 10 percent of all dogs in Australia. This complete guide covers symptoms, severity levels, treatment options, lifestyle changes and real community case studies — everything you need to help your dog live comfortably.

💡 Key Takeaway

Heart disease is common but manageable for your dog's quality of life. With early diagnosis, proper medication, and lifestyle adjustments, most senior dogs with heart disease can live active, happy lives for years after diagnosis.

Types of Heart Disease in Dogs

Heart disease in dogs takes several forms, affecting the heart's ability to pump blood effectively. Degenerative Valve Disease (MVD) is most common in small-to-medium breeds (Cavalier KC, Poodles, Chihuahuas). The heart valves wear out and leak blood. Dilated Cardiomyopathy (DCM) affects large breeds (Dobermans, Boxers, Great Danes). The heart muscle weakens and struggles to pump. Heart Murmurs are abnormal sounds during heartbeat cycles caused by turbulent blood flow and should always be evaluated.

Heart disease is especially common in senior dogs over 7 years old. Up to 75 percent of senior dogs develop some form of heart murmur. Annual vet heart checks are essential for early detection. While most forms cannot be cured, they can be managed effectively. Early diagnosis is critical — the earlier treatment begins, the better the long-term outcome.

❤️🐕
Mitral valve disease is the most common heart condition in small breed dogs. Regular stethoscope exams can detect murmurs early.

Heart Disease Severity Levels

Heart disease severity is typically classified by the ACVIM staging system. Here is a simplified overview:

🟢 Stage B1–B2 (Mild)
Often no symptoms. Heart murmur found during routine vet check with a stethoscope.
Regular monitoring ± early medication
~$80–150/month (monitoring)
🟡 Stage C (Moderate)
Symptoms appear: cough, reduced stamina, rapid breathing. Medication usually starts here.
Vet medication + diet change (low sodium)
~$150–300/month
🔴 Stage D (Severe)
Significant symptoms: fluid buildup, labored breathing at rest. Requires full cardiac care.
Full cardiac care + palliative management
~$500–1,000+/month

Heart Disease Care & Treatment

Regular Vet Checkups and Scans

Annual heart checks including stethoscope exam, chest X-rays, and echocardiograms help track disease progression. Even a 6-month checkup cycle can make a significant difference — early detection of changes allows medication adjustments before symptoms worsen.

Heart Medication

Common medications include ACE inhibitors (enalapril, benazepril) to reduce cardiac workload, diuretics (furosemide) to manage fluid buildup, and pimobendan to strengthen heart contractions. Never adjust medication without vet consultation. Consistent daily dosing is essential.

Controlled Exercise

Short, gentle walks are better than long or strenuous exercise. Avoid hot weather and strenuous activity that puts stress on the heart. Watch for signs of fatigue — if your dog lies down mid-walk, it's time to head home. Swimming is generally not recommended for dogs with significant heart disease.

Low-Sodium Diet

Reducing salt intake minimizes fluid retention and reduces strain on the heart. Prescription cardiac diets (Hills h/d, Royal Canin Cardiac) are formulated with controlled sodium levels. Avoid salty human foods, commercial treats, and table scraps. Check ingredient labels carefully.

Keep Calm and Comfortable

Stress worsens heart symptoms. Provide a calm, stable home environment with non-slip flooring, easy access to food and water, and a comfortable resting area. Avoid exciting situations like boarding kennels or noisy gatherings. A calm, stable home makes a real difference to your dog's quality of life.
