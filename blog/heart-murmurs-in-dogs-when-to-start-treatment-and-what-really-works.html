Is Your Dog's Heart Murmur Something to Worry About?

If your vet mentioned a heart murmur at your dog's last check-up, you're probably feeling a bit anxious. It's a common worry among Aussie dog owners, especially if you have a Cavalier King Charles Spaniel, a Cocker Spaniel, or a small breed like a Maltese or Dachshund.

But here's the good news: not every heart murmur needs urgent treatment. In fact, many dogs live long, happy lives with a murmur, especially if it's caught early.

Let's talk about what the latest research says about one specific stage of heart disease—Stage B2—and how a medication called pimobendan can make a real difference.

What Exactly Is Stage B2 Heart Disease?

Imagine your dog's heart as a pump with four doors (valves). In myxomatous mitral valve disease (MMVD), one of those doors—the mitral valve—starts to leak. Over time, that leak causes the heart to enlarge as it works harder to push blood around.

Stage B2 is the "silent" stage. Your dog seems perfectly fine—no coughing, no heavy breathing, no collapsing. But on an ultrasound (echocardiogram), the vet can see that the heart is getting bigger and the leak is significant.

It's like discovering your car's engine is wearing out before it starts making noises. You have a chance to act before real trouble begins.

The Study That Changed How Vets Treat Heart Disease

A recent study looked at dogs with Stage B2 MMVD who were given only pimobendan (no other heart meds). The results were impressive:

  • The left side of the heart actually got smaller (reduced cardiac remodeling)
  • The heart pumped more efficiently (improved fractional shortening and ejection fraction)
  • Dogs stayed symptom-free for longer

This means starting treatment early can delay or even prevent congestive heart failure.

How Does Pimobendan Work? (In Plain English)

Pimobendan does two things at once:

  1. It strengthens the heart's squeeze (like giving your heart a gentle caffeine boost)
  2. It relaxes blood vessels so the heart doesn't have to push against so much resistance

Think of it like helping a tired swimmer by both giving them more energy and reducing the current they're swimming against. The result? The heart doesn't have to work as hard, and the enlargement can slow down or even reverse.

Real-Life Example: Meet Charlie the Cavalier

Charlie is an 8-year-old Cavalier King Charles Spaniel from Melbourne. During a routine check-up, his vet heard a murmur and recommended an ultrasound. The results showed Stage B2 MMVD—a moderately enlarged heart with a significant leak.

Charlie's owner, Sarah, was worried, but Charlie seemed perfectly happy chasing balls and begging for treats. The vet started Charlie on pimobendan twice a day.

A year later, another ultrasound showed that Charlie's heart dimensions had actually improved. The leak was stable, and Charlie still had no symptoms. Sarah says, "I just give him his tablet with a bit of peanut butter, and he's his usual cheeky self."

What About Dosage? How Much Does My Dog Need?

In the study, dogs received 0.2 to 0.3 mg per kilogram of body weight, twice daily. That's about one tablet for a 10 kg dog, but your vet will calculate the exact dose based on your dog's weight and condition.

Important tips:
  • Give pimobendan consistently—either always with food or always without, as your vet advises
  • Don't skip doses; set a reminder on your phone
  • Never double up if you forget a dose—just give the next one at the usual time

A Word of Caution: Not All Heart Disease Is the Same

Pimobendan is NOT safe for every type of heart problem. It should never be used in dogs with hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM), a condition where the heart muscle is too thick. In those dogs, pimobendan can actually make things worse.

That's why it's so important to get a proper diagnosis (usually with an ultrasound) before starting any heart medication. Don't just rely on a stethoscope alone.

Busting a Common Myth: "My Dog Has a Murmur, So He Needs Meds Now"

Not true! Many dogs have innocent murmurs or very early-stage disease that doesn't need treatment. Only Stage B2 (with significant heart enlargement on ultrasound) is the point where pimobendan is recommended.

Your vet will tell you if your dog has reached that stage. Don't panic if you hear the word "murmur"—it doesn't automatically mean medication.

What Can You Do Tonight?

  1. Know your dog's breed risk – Cavaliers, Cocker Spaniels, Dachshunds, and other small breeds are more prone to MMVD
  2. Listen to your dog's breathing – A normal resting breathing rate is under 30 breaths per minute. If it's consistently higher, mention it to your vet
  3. Keep up with annual check-ups – Especially after age 6 for small breeds
  4. Ask your vet about an ultrasound – If your dog has a murmur, an echocardiogram is the best way to know the stage

The Bottom Line

Early detection and treatment of Stage B2 MMVD with pimobendan can give your dog years of happy, active life—without ever reaching the coughing, struggling stage of heart failure. It's not a cure, but it's a powerful tool to slow the disease down.

If your dog has a heart murmur, don't ignore it—but don't panic either. Have a chat with your vet, get the right tests, and if treatment is needed, know that you have safe, effective options.

Your dog's heart is in good hands. And with a bit of attention and the right medication, you can keep that tail wagging for years to come.
