Is Your Dog’s Cough More Than Just a Bad Habit? Spotting Hidden Heart and Lung Trouble

Hey there, fellow Aussie dog lover! 👋

We all know that sound—the dry, honking cough that seems to come out of nowhere, especially at night or after a game of fetch. Maybe you’ve put it down to “just a sensitive throat” or “getting older.” But what if that cough is actually a sign of something deeper going on inside your little mate’s chest?

Today, we’re going to talk about a sneaky condition called pulmonary hypertension (PH)—or high blood pressure in the lungs—and how it often tags along with a very common heart problem in small dogs: degenerative mitral valve disease (DMVD). Don’t worry, no complicated Latin here—just plain English and practical tips for your furry family member.

---

What’s Really Going On?

Imagine your dog’s heart has four rooms. The mitral valve is like a door between two rooms on the left side. In DMVD, that door starts to wear out and leak. Over time, the leak can cause pressure to build up, and that pressure can travel all the way back to the lungs. That’s when we call it pulmonary hypertension.

Here’s the kicker: PH is really common in dogs with advanced DMVD. In fact, one study found that about 4 out of 10 dogs with stage C DMVD (that’s the stage where heart failure symptoms appear) also have PH. And if left unchecked, it can cut your dog’s quality time with you short.

But the good news? Early detection can make a massive difference.

---

The Signs You Might Miss (But Shouldn’t)

PH can be quiet at first. Your dog might seem fine—happy, eating well, wagging tail. But as it progresses, here’s what to watch for:

  • A dry, persistent cough that gets worse at night or after exercise
  • Breathing that’s faster or more laboured than usual
  • Getting tired more quickly on walks or during play
  • A swollen belly (from fluid build-up)
  • Blue-ish gums or tongue (this is an emergency!)

> Real-life story: A 10-year-old toy poodle came into the vet with a cough that the owner thought was just “tracheal sensitivity.” Turns out, X-rays showed an enlarged heart and thickened lung arteries. An ultrasound confirmed DMVD with PH. After starting medication (including a drug to lower the pressure in her lungs), she was coughing way less within two weeks and had her energy back!

---

How Vets Find the Problem (Without Fancy Gadgets)

You might think you need a specialist and expensive equipment to spot PH. But your regular vet can often pick up clues from a simple chest X-ray and an ultrasound of the heart.

On an X-ray: The vet looks for a “bulge” in the main artery to the lungs (think of it like a thick hose that’s under too much pressure). They also check if the lung arteries look bigger than the nearby veins. On an ultrasound: A smaller-than-normal left ventricle (the main pumping chamber) can be a red flag that the right side of the heart is working overtime.

If your vet sees these signs, they’ll probably suggest a referral to a heart specialist for a full diagnosis. But the key is—you don’t need to wait for a specialist to start asking questions.

---

What You Can Do Tonight (Actionable Advice)

#### 1. Keep a “Cough Diary”

Write down when the cough happens, how long it lasts, and what your dog was doing. This info is gold for your vet.

#### 2. Watch the Belly

Gently feel your dog’s tummy each day. If it feels tight or looks bigger than usual, take a photo and show your vet.

#### 3. Don’t Panic, But Don’t Delay

If your dog has a new cough or seems more tired, book a vet visit within a week. If they’re struggling to breathe or their gums look blue, head straight to the emergency vet.

#### 4. Check Their Diet

  • No extra salt! Avoid ham, cheese, and salty treats. They can make fluid retention worse.
  • Don’t restrict water unless your vet says so. Diuretics can cause dehydration.
  • Consider a prescription heart diet that’s low in sodium and rich in taurine.

#### 5. Ask About Supplements

Some supplements may help support the heart, but always check with your vet first.

  • Taurine: 250–500 mg per 10 kg body weight, 2–3 times a day
  • L-carnitine: 50–100 mg per kg, 2–3 times a day
  • CoQ10: 1–2 mg per kg, once or twice a day
  • Fish oil (Omega-3): 50–100 mg of EPA/DHA per kg, once a day

---

Busting a Common Myth

“My small dog is just getting old and coughs—that’s normal for heart disease.”

Not true! While small breeds like Poodles, Pomeranians, Chihuahuas, and Cavaliers are prone to heart murmurs, a new cough or breathing change is not something to accept. It could be a sign that PH has developed, and with the right medication, many dogs feel much better.

---

The Bottom Line

Pulmonary hypertension is a serious complication of common heart disease in small dogs, but it’s not a death sentence. With regular check-ups (every 3–6 months if your dog has known heart issues), a good relationship with your vet, and a few simple changes at home, you can help your best mate stay comfortable and happy for longer.

So next time you hear that cough, don’t just shrug it off. Give your vet a call—it could be the first step to giving your dog a whole new lease on life.
