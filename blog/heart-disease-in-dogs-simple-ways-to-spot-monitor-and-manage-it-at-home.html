Is Your Dog’s Heart Ok? A Friendly Guide for Aussie Pet Owners

G’day, fellow dog lover! If you’ve ever noticed your mate getting puffed after a short walk or coughing more than usual, you might wonder if their heart is doing alright. Heart disease in dogs is more common than we think, especially as they get older. But the good news? There are simple ways to keep an eye on things and help your pup live a happy, active life.

Let’s break it down in plain English—no fancy Latin words, just practical advice you can use tonight.

What to Watch For: Early Signs of Heart Trouble

Your dog can’t tell you when something’s wrong, but their body will give clues. Look out for:

  • Coughing, especially at night or after exercise
  • Getting tired quickly on walks
  • Heavy or fast breathing when resting
  • Fainting or collapsing (even for a second)
  • A swollen belly (from fluid buildup)

These signs might be subtle at first. For example, a 9-year-old Labrador we saw started coughing and slowing down on walks. His X-rays showed an enlarged heart, but his lungs looked fine. A simple blood test revealed high inflammation markers, and an ultrasound confirmed dilated cardiomyopathy (a weak heart muscle). The earlier we catch it, the better we can manage it.

Simple Blood Tests That Help Your Vet

Your vet might recommend a few blood tests to check your dog’s heart health. Don’t worry—they’re straightforward and give a lot of info.

What’s CRP and Why Does It Matter?

CRP (C-reactive protein) is a marker of inflammation. In dogs with heart disease, CRP can rise even before any changes show up on X-rays or heart scans. It’s like an early warning light. For example, a 5-year-old mixed breed from a shelter had a mild cough and high CRP. An ultrasound showed pulmonary hypertension (high blood pressure in the lungs) from heartworms. After treatment, his CRP went back to normal and so did his energy.

Important: High CRP doesn’t always mean heart disease—it can go up with any infection or injury. But combined with other tests, it’s a great screening tool.

Electrolytes: The Salts That Keep the Heart Beating

Electrolytes like potassium and sodium are crucial for heart function. Some heart medications, especially diuretics (water pills), can throw them out of balance. Common issues:

  • Low potassium (hypokalaemia): Can cause muscle weakness, irregular heartbeats, or even sudden collapse
  • Low sodium (hyponatraemia): Often happens with fluid retention and heart failure
  • High potassium (hyperkalaemia): May occur if kidneys aren’t working well

Regular blood tests help your vet adjust medications and prevent dangerous swings. Never change your dog’s diuretic dose without talking to your vet first.

The Myth About CK (Creatine Kinase)

You might have heard that CK is a heart damage marker. Actually, total CK goes up with ANY muscle damage—like from a romp at the beach or a fall. It’s not specific to the heart. Vets now rely on better tests like troponin and natriuretic peptides for accurate heart assessment.

How to Prevent Heart-Kidney Problems (Cardiorenal Syndrome)

Heart and kidney health are closely linked. When the heart struggles, kidneys can suffer too. To protect both:

  • Get regular blood work to check kidney values like creatinine and phosphorus
  • Avoid overusing diuretics—they can dehydrate your dog and upset electrolytes
  • Keep your dog well-hydrated with fresh water always available
  • Treat heart disease early with your vet’s guidance

Real-Life Success Story: Max the Labrador

Max, a 9-year-old Labrador, came in with a cough and low energy. His chest X-rays showed an enlarged heart but no lung changes. Blood tests revealed high CRP (35 mg/L, normal <10). An ultrasound confirmed dilated cardiomyopathy. With medication (pimobendan and diuretics) and regular monitoring, Max’s CRP dropped, his cough improved, and he was back to chasing balls in the park within weeks.

What You Can Do Tonight

  1. Check your dog’s breathing rate when they’re asleep. Count breaths for 15 seconds and multiply by 4. Normal is under 30 breaths per minute. If it’s consistently higher, mention it to your vet.
  2. Note any coughing after exercise or at night. A soft, occasional cough might be normal, but a persistent one needs checking.
  3. Keep a diary of any changes in energy, appetite, or behaviour. This helps your vet spot patterns.
  4. Never give human heart meds to your dog—they can be toxic.
  5. Book a vet visit if you’re worried. Early detection makes a huge difference.

The Bottom Line

Heart disease in dogs is manageable, especially when caught early. Simple blood tests like CRP and electrolytes give your vet powerful clues. Combine them with regular check-ups, a healthy diet, and plenty of love, and you’ll give your mate the best chance at a long, happy life.
