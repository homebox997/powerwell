Is Your Dog’s Heart Murmur a Big Deal? What Every Aussie Owner Should Know About Early Heart Disease

You know that moment at the vet when they say, “I can hear a little heart murmur”? Your mind immediately jumps to worst-case scenarios, and you start Googling like crazy. But take a breath, mate. Heart murmurs in dogs—especially small breeds—are incredibly common, and many dogs live long, happy lives with them.

Let’s talk about the most common heart disease in dogs: myxomatous mitral valve disease (MMVD). Sounds fancy, but it’s basically a wear-and-tear problem with the heart’s valve. And guess what? Chances are, your little mate might have it one day.

What Is MMVD, Really?

Imagine your dog’s heart as a house with four rooms. The mitral valve is like a door between two rooms on the left side. Over time, that door gets a bit floppy and doesn’t close properly. A little blood leaks back through—that’s the murmur your vet hears.

Now, here’s the good news: in the early stages, many dogs show zero signs of being sick. They still zoom around the park, beg for treats, and snore on the couch. But it’s still important to know what stage they’re in, because that decides if they need medication.

The Stages: B1 vs B2

Vets use a staging system (A, B1, B2, C, D) to figure out where your dog’s at. For most dogs diagnosed early, they’ll be in stage B—which means they have a murmur but no heart failure symptoms.

  • Stage B1: The heart has a murmur, but the chambers aren’t big enough to need medication yet. Your vet will say, “Let’s just keep an eye on it.”
  • Stage B2: The left side of the heart has enlarged enough that starting treatment is recommended. This is when drugs like pimobendan (Vetmedin) often come into the picture.

A recent study looked at 60 dogs in stages B1 and B2. They found that while some electrical changes in the heart were different (like longer P waves on an ECG), the dogs’ overall heart rhythm stability was still good. That means even with an enlarged heart, your dog’s body is doing a pretty impressive job of compensating.

Myth-Busting: “Stage B2 Means Dangerous Arrhythmias”

One of the biggest worries owners have is that an enlarged heart will cause scary, dangerous heart rhythms. But the research says that’s not necessarily true. In fact, the study found no significant difference in the risk of arrhythmias between B1 and B2 dogs. The nervous system’s balance (sympathetic vs parasympathetic) stayed stable. So don’t panic—many stage B2 dogs remain stable for years.

When Should You Start Treatment?

Here’s the simple rule: Stage B1 = no medication needed. Stage B2 = usually time to start treatment.

Why wait? Because the heart isn’t enlarged enough yet to benefit from drugs. Starting too early can even have downsides. Your vet will use an echocardiogram (heart ultrasound) to measure exactly how big the chambers are and decide when it’s time.

Charlie’s Story: A Real Aussie Pooch

Take Charlie, a 9-year-old Cavalier King Charles Spaniel from Melbourne. He was fit as a fiddle, no coughing, no tiredness. But during his annual check-up, the vet heard a murmur. An ultrasound showed his left heart chambers were enlarged—stage B2.

Charlie’s owner was terrified. But his vet explained that many dogs in this stage live normally for years with just a little pill twice a day. Charlie started on pimobendan, kept his daily walks, and is still going strong. The key was catching it early.

Practical Tips You Can Use Tonight

#### 1. Get Regular Check-ups

MMVD is sneaky—it often shows no symptoms until it’s advanced. Annual vet visits with a listen to the heart can catch it early. For at-risk breeds (Cavaliers, Dachshunds, Miniature Poodles), consider a heart check every 6-12 months after age 7.

#### 2. Don’t Over-Restrict Salt

You might think a low-salt diet is best for heart dogs, but going too strict can backfire. Severe salt restriction can actually make the heart work harder. Stick to a balanced diet with moderate sodium. Your vet can recommend a good commercial heart-health food.

#### 3. Consider Omega-3 Supplements

Fish oil rich in EPA and DHA can support heart health. A typical dose is about 40–100 mg per kg of your dog’s body weight per day, given with food. It’s anti-inflammatory and may help slow disease progression. Always check with your vet first.

#### 4. Watch for Subtle Signs

Even in early stages, keep an eye out for:

  • Coughing (especially at night or after exercise)
  • Tiring more easily on walks
  • Heavy or rapid breathing when resting
  • Restlessness or pacing at night

If you notice any of these, book a vet visit sooner rather than later.

The Bottom Line

A heart murmur doesn’t mean your dog’s life is over. With early detection, proper staging, and sensible management, many dogs live happily for years. The key is knowing the stage and working with your vet to decide when to start treatment.

So next time your vet mentions a murmur, don’t panic. Ask them: “What stage is it? B1 or B2?” That one question will tell you everything you need to know.
