Is Your Dog Slowing Down? It Might Be More Than Just Getting Older

We all know that feeling – you grab the lead for a walk, and instead of the usual excited bounce, your dog just looks at you from their bed. Or maybe they’re not jumping onto the couch like they used to, and stairs seem like a mountain. It’s easy to put it down to age, but sometimes it’s a sign of something else: joint pain.

Don’t worry, we’re not here to give you a scary diagnosis. We’re here to help you spot the early signs and give you practical, down-to-earth tips to keep your best mate happy and moving. Think of this as a chat over a cuppa, not a lecture from the vet school.

What to Look For: The Subtle Signs of Sore Joints

Dogs are tough – they don’t always yelp when they’re sore. Instead, they show us in quieter ways. Here are a few things to watch for:

  • Stiffness after resting: Especially first thing in the morning or after a long nap. They might take a few steps like they’re ‘warming up’.
  • Less enthusiasm for jumping: If your dog used to launch onto the bed or into the car but now hesitates, that’s a big clue.
  • A bunny-hop run: When running, do their back legs move together like a rabbit? That’s often a sign of hip discomfort.
  • Lagging behind on walks: Not just because they want to sniff everything – if they’re dropping behind more than usual, it could be their joints.
  • Licking or chewing at a spot: They might focus on a particular leg or joint, almost like they’re trying to massage it.
Important: If you see any of these signs, it’s always a good idea to have a chat with your vet. They can check things out properly.

The Good News: You Can Help Prevent Joint Problems

The best time to start looking after your dog’s joints is before they ever have a problem. And it’s simpler than you think.

Keep the Weight Off (It’s the Biggest Thing You Can Do)

This one is huge. Clinical studies show that overweight dogs are much more likely to get arthritis. In fact, one study found that keeping Labrador Retrievers lean cut their risk of hip arthritis by nearly half. That’s amazing. So, keep your dog at a healthy weight – it’s the single best thing for their joints. Talk to your vet about what ‘ideal weight’ means for your breed.

Low-Impact Exercise is Your Best Friend

You don’t need to run marathons with your dog. Gentle, regular exercise is perfect. Think:

  • Swimming: It’s brilliant – no impact, but great for building muscle.
  • Leash walks on grass or dirt: Softer surfaces are kinder than concrete.
  • Avoiding hard pavement for long runs: Especially if your dog is still growing or already a bit stiff.

Make Your Home Joint-Friendly

  • Ramps for furniture: A simple ramp for the couch or car can save a lot of wear and tear.
  • Non-slip flooring: If you have tiles or polished floors, put down some rugs or mats. Slipping is bad for joints and scary for your dog.
  • Orthopaedic beds: A good supportive bed makes a huge difference for sore hips and elbows.

What About Supplements? A Quick Guide

You’ve probably seen glucosamine and chondroitin at the pet shop. They can help, but dosing matters.

  • Glucosamine: The typical dose is about 20-25 mg per pound of body weight each day, split into two doses. For a 50-pound dog (like a medium Staffy or Kelpie), that’s about 500-625 mg twice a day.
  • Chondroitin: Often given at 15-20 mg per pound daily.
  • Always use vet-formulated products: Human supplements can contain xylitol (which is toxic to dogs) or have the wrong concentration. Ask your vet for a recommendation.

The Truth About Pain Relief (And What NOT to Do)

Never, ever give your dog human painkillers like ibuprofen, naproxen or paracetamol. This can be fatal. Even a single tablet can cause severe stomach ulcers, kidney failure, or liver damage. If your dog needs pain relief, your vet has safe, dog-specific options like NSAIDs (non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs). Always go to your vet for these.

A Real-Life Aussie Story: How Max the Labrador Got His Bounce Back

Meet Max, a 7-year-old Labrador. He was a big boy – 95 kilos (20 kilos over his ideal weight). He had hip arthritis and could barely climb stairs. His owner started a strict diet (measured meals, swapped treats for carrots and green beans) and added daily swimming. Over six months, Max lost 18 kilos. Within eight weeks, his lameness improved, and he started jumping onto the couch again. It’s not magic – it’s just good, consistent care.

The Bottom Line

Joint pain in dogs is common, but it doesn’t have to mean the end of fun walks and playtime. By watching for early signs, keeping your dog lean, using gentle exercise, and talking to your vet, you can help your mate stay comfortable and active for years to come. And remember: if you’re ever unsure, your vet is just a phone call away.
