My Dog Is Limping: What to Do Right Now (Aussie Owner's Guide)

By PawWell Editorial · 2026-07-23 · joint

My Dog Is Limping: What to Do Right Now (Aussie Owner's Guide)

Picture this: you're at the local off-leash park, your pup is having a blast chasing a ball, and then suddenly—they come back holding up a leg. Your heart sinks. "Is it broken? A sprain? Something worse?"

It happens to the best of us. Limping is one of the top reasons Aussie pet owners rush to the vet. But before you panic, let's break down what you need to know, what you can do tonight, and when to call the emergency vet.

First Things First: Stay Calm and Observe

If your dog is limping, take a deep breath. Watch them for a minute. Are they putting any weight on the leg? Are they still happy and eating? Or do they look sick and miserable?

Here's a quick checklist:

  • Can they walk at all?
  • Is the leg hot or swollen?
  • Do they have a fever? (A normal dog temp is around 38°C; anything above 38.5°C is a red flag)
  • Are they eating and drinking normally?
  • Did the limp come on suddenly after a run, or slowly over days?

The Golden Rule: NEVER Give Human Painkillers

This is the most important thing I'll say today. If your dog is limping, **do not** reach for your own medicine cabinet. Ibuprofen (Nurofen), paracetamol (Panadol), naproxen (Aleve)—these are highly toxic to dogs. They can cause stomach ulcers, kidney failure, or even death.

Even aspirin is risky unless your vet specifically tells you the dose. The safest move? Restrict your dog's activity and call your vet for advice.

When It's an Emergency (Read This Carefully)

Most limps are not life-threatening, but some are. You need to get to a vet immediately if your dog has:

  • **A fever** (over 38.5°C or 101.3°F)
  • **Refuses to walk** or put any weight on the leg
  • **Looks generally sick**—lethargic, not eating, vomiting
  • **Heat and swelling** around a joint
  • **A known injury** like a fall or car accident

These signs could point to **septic arthritis** (a serious joint infection) or **osteomyelitis** (bone infection). Both are medical emergencies. In one real case, a 2-year-old Labrador named Max started limping after a play session. X-rays showed no fracture, but blood tests revealed an infection. He needed joint flushing and IV antibiotics. Don't wait if you see these red flags.

The Most Common Cause: Trauma

Believe it or not, the number one reason dogs limp is plain old physical trauma. That means:

  • Slipping on the back deck
  • A bad landing after jumping for a frisbee
  • Colliding with another dog at the park
  • Stepping in a hole in the backyard

Prevention is your best friend here. Keep your dog at a healthy weight (less stress on joints), supervise rough play, and avoid high-impact activities on hard surfaces—especially for puppies and senior dogs. Swimming is a fantastic low-impact exercise that builds muscle without jarring the joints.

The "Painless" Limp Myth

A common myth is that if a limp doesn't seem painful, it's not serious. That's simply not true. A painless limp can be caused by:

  • **Hip dysplasia** (common in labs, retrievers, and shepherds)
  • **Leg length differences**
  • **Early-stage bone cancer** (osteosarcoma)

If your dog has been limping for more than a day or two, even if they seem fine, get them checked. Early diagnosis makes a huge difference.

Knee Pain? It Might Be the Hip

Here's a surprising fact vets see all the time: a dog limping and pointing to their knee might actually have a hip problem. This is especially true in young dogs and puppies. Conditions like hip dysplasia can show up as knee-area lameness. So don't assume it's just a sprain—a thorough vet exam and X-rays are often needed.

What About Supplements?

If your dog has chronic joint issues or is prone to limping from arthritis, glucosamine supplements can help. A standard dose is about 20-30 mg per pound of body weight per day, split into two doses. For a 50-pound dog, that's around 500-750 mg twice daily.

**Important:** Always choose a product made for pets. Human supplements can contain xylitol, which is toxic to dogs. And remember, supplements are not emergency treatments—they're for long-term joint support.

What You Can Do Tonight

If your dog is limping but otherwise happy, eating, and has no fever:

1. **Rest them**—keep them indoors, no running, no jumping, no dog park

2. **Check the paw**—look for cuts, thorns, or something stuck between the toes

3. **Apply a cold pack** (wrapped in a towel) to any swollen area for 10-15 minutes

4. **Call your vet in the morning** for advice

If the limp doesn't improve in 24-48 hours, or if it gets worse, book a vet appointment.

The Bottom Line

Limping is scary, but most cases are manageable with rest and vet care. Just remember:

  • No human painkillers
  • Watch for fever and refusal to walk
  • Get a vet check if it persists
  • Prevention is key—keep your dog fit, lean, and supervised

You know your dog better than anyone. If something feels off, trust your gut and call the vet. They're there to help.

Stay safe, and give your pup an extra pat from us.
