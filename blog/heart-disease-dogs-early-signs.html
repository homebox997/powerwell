Heart Disease in Dogs: How to Spot Early Signs and Keep Your Pup Safe

By PawWell Editorial · 2026-07-23 · heart

Why Your Dog's Breathing Matters More Than You Think

Hey there, fellow dog lover! If you're reading this, chances are you've got a sweet senior pup or a small breed dog that's been diagnosed with a heart murmur. And let's be honest—when the vet says "heart disease," it can feel overwhelming. But here's the good news: there's something simple you can do at home that could literally save your dog's life.

I'm talking about counting their breathing. Yep, just watching them sleep and counting how many breaths they take in a minute. It's that easy, and it's one of the most powerful tools you have to spot trouble before it becomes an emergency.

What's 'Normal' Breathing for a Dog?

First things first: a healthy, resting dog—one that's lying down, relaxed, and not panting—should take **fewer than 30 to 40 breaths per minute**. Think of it like this: if your dog is snoozing on the couch, you should see their chest rise and fall about once every two seconds.

Now, if you notice their breathing is consistently **above 40 to 50 breaths per minute** while resting, that's a red flag. It could mean fluid is building up in their lungs, which is a sign of congestive heart failure. Catching this early gives your vet a head start on treatment.

How to Check Your Dog's Respiratory Rate at Home

Here's your step-by-step guide, Aussie-style:

1. **Wait for your dog to be truly asleep or deeply resting**—not just lying down but awake. A sleeping dog gives you the most accurate reading.

2. **Set a timer for 60 seconds** (or 30 seconds and double it).

3. **Watch their chest or belly move**. One breath = one inhale + one exhale.

4. **Write it down**. Keep a little notebook or use your phone to track it daily.

**Pro tip:** Do this at the same time every day, like after their afternoon nap. Consistency is key.

Real Story: Meet Charlie, the 12-Year-Old Toy Breed Who Beat the Odds

Let me tell you about Charlie, a 3.3 kg little bloke who came into the hospital with severe breathing trouble. His respiratory rate was 68 breaths per minute—way too fast. He was struggling, and his owners were terrified.

But here's the good news: with oxygen therapy and injectable medications, within 12 hours his breathing had dropped to 40 breaths per minute. He went home a few days later. That's the power of early detection and quick action.

What About That Loud Heart Murmur?

You might have heard that a loud heart murmur means your dog is in big danger. Well, that's not quite true. Research shows that while a loud murmur might mean your dog is more likely to need re-hospitalisation, it **doesn't predict death** during that hospital stay. So don't panic if your pup has a loud murmur—it's just one piece of the puzzle.

The First 12 Hours Are Critical

Vets have a special tool called the Tufts Dyspnea Score (just a fancy way of measuring how hard your dog is breathing). And here's what they've found: **if your dog's breathing improves significantly within the first 12 hours of treatment, their chances of going home are much higher**. So if your pup is ever hospitalised for breathing trouble, ask the vet how their score is changing.

What Raises the Risk After a Heart Emergency?

If your dog has been hospitalised for fluid in the lungs (called cardiogenic pulmonary edema), there are a few things that can increase the risk of problems in the next two months:

  • Needing strong heart medications during their hospital stay
  • Having a high respiratory rate (over 40-50 breaths per minute) in the first 12 hours
  • Low blood pressure (systolic below 90 mmHg) when they arrived

These are all things your vet will monitor closely, so don't stress—just be aware.

Diet: What NOT to Feed Your Heart Dog

Okay, let's talk about food. Dogs with heart disease should **avoid high-sodium foods**. That means:

  • No table scraps (especially chips, sausages, or anything salty)
  • No cheese or processed meats
  • Limit commercial treats (check the label for salt content)

Instead, opt for low-sodium treats like fresh carrot sticks, green beans, or plain cooked chicken (no salt added). Always check with your vet before making big diet changes.

Supplements: A Word of Caution

You might have heard about supplements like taurine, omega-3s, or CoQ10 for heart health. While some can be helpful, **there's no standard dosage** from this study. Always talk to your vet before starting any supplement—some can interact with medications.

When to See the Vet (Like, Right Now)

If you notice any of these signs, call your vet immediately:

  • Breathing that's consistently above 40 breaths per minute at rest
  • Coughing, especially at night or after exercise
  • Gums that look pale or blue
  • Fainting or collapsing
  • Reluctance to walk or play
  • Swollen belly (fluid buildup)

The Bottom Line

Heart disease in dogs is serious, but it's not a death sentence. With a little bit of home monitoring—just counting breaths while they sleep—you can catch problems early and give your pup the best chance at a long, happy life. You've got this, and your dog is lucky to have you.
