Your Dog's Back Pain: Spotting Early Signs of Arthritis & Spondylosis in Aussie Pups

By PawWell Editorial · 2026-07-23 · joint

Is Your Dog Slowing Down? It Might Be More Than Just 'Getting Older'

We all know that heart-sinking feeling when our best mate starts to slow down on walks, or hesitates before jumping onto the couch. It's easy to put it down to 'just getting older' – especially if you've got a Labrador who's hit their golden years, or a German Shepherd who's always been a bit stiff in the mornings.

But here's the thing: that stiffness, that hesitation, that occasional limp – it could be a sign of something more, like **hip dysplasia** or **spondylosis** (which is just a fancy name for arthritis along the spine). And the good news is, the earlier you catch it, the better you can manage it and keep your dog comfortable and happy for years to come.

Let's break it down in plain Aussie English – no Latin, no medical jargon, just real talk for real dog owners.

What Are We Actually Talking About?

Hip Dysplasia

Imagine a loose, wobbly ball-and-socket joint where the hip should fit snugly. That's hip dysplasia. It's a common developmental condition, especially in large-breed puppies like Labradors, Golden Retrievers, and German Shepherds. It can cause a non-painful limp early on, but if left unchecked, it can lead to arthritis, pain, and chronic lameness down the track.

Spondylosis

This is arthritis of the spine. Think of it like the body trying to 'splint' a sore back by growing extra bone along the vertebrae. It's common in older dogs (and even in cats!) and can cause stiffness, pain, and eventually nerve issues if it gets severe. The key is to spot it early.

Early Warning Signs: What to Watch For

Your dog can't tell you their back hurts, but they show you in their behaviour. Here are the classic signs to keep an eye out for:

1. The 'Slow to Get Up' Morning Routine

If your dog is stiff when they first get up, especially after a sleep or a long lie-down, that's a classic sign of joint discomfort. They might take a few steps with a 'bunny hop' (both back legs together) before they loosen up.

2. Reluctance to Jump or Climb

Does your once-energetic kelpie suddenly hesitate before jumping onto the ute tray? Or does your labrador need a little boost onto the bed? That's a red flag.

3. Changes in Gait (How They Walk)

Look for a swaying or waddling walk in the back end. They might also 'knuckle' their back paws (walk on the top of their foot) or drag their toes. This can be a sign of nerve compression from spondylosis.

4. Stiff Neck or Head Tilt

A dog with neck arthritis might hold their head low, be reluctant to look up at you, or cry out when you touch their neck. They might also have a stiff, stilted walk.

5. Loss of Muscle Mass

As the spine or hips become painful, dogs often 'guard' the area by not using those muscles properly. You might notice the muscles along their back or back legs becoming thinner over time.

What Can You Do About It? Practical Steps You Can Take Tonight

Alright, so you've spotted some signs. What now? Here's your actionable checklist:

1. First Things First: See Your Vet

Don't guess. A proper diagnosis is key. Your vet might recommend screening X-rays like PennHIP or OFA evaluation (for hips) or an MRI (for spine issues). But here's a common myth: **not every neck or back pain needs an X-ray**. If your dog just has a sore neck without any nerve signs (like weakness or a funny walk), a physical exam and history might be enough.

2. Manage That Weight – Seriously

This is the single biggest thing you can control. Extra weight puts massive pressure on already sore joints. Think of it this way: every extra kilogram on a dog is like carrying a heavy backpack all day. **If your dog is even slightly overweight, getting them lean can dramatically reduce pain.**

**Actionable tip:** Swap out half their dinner for some steamed green beans or pumpkin. It's low-calorie, filling, and most dogs love it.

3. Modify Exercise (Not Stop It!)

  • **Avoid hard surfaces:** No more running on concrete footpaths. Stick to grass, sand, or dirt trails.
  • **Keep walks short and frequent:** Three 10-minute walks are better than one 30-minute walk.
  • **Swimming is a game-changer:** If you have a pool or can get to a dog-friendly beach, swimming is amazing for building muscle without jarring the joints.
  • **No more 'fetch' on hard ground:** That repetitive sprinting and stopping is tough on hips and spines.

4. Make Your Home Joint-Friendly

  • **Ramps, not jumps:** Put a ramp at the back door or for the ute. Even a cheap wooden plank with some grip tape works.
  • **Orthopaedic bedding:** A thick memory foam bed (not a thin cushion) can make a world of difference for a sore back.
  • **Raise their food bowls:** This reduces strain on the neck and shoulders.

5. Consider Supplements (But Pick Wisely)

You'll see a million joint supplements at the pet shop. The ones with the best evidence are:

  • **Green-lipped mussel powder** (NZ green-lipped mussel is fantastic)
  • **Omega-3 fatty acids** (fish oil)
  • **Glucosamine and chondroitin** (good for cartilage support)

Always check with your vet before starting anything new – some supplements can interact with other meds.

When Should You Really Worry? The 'Red Flags' That Need Urgent Vet Care

If your dog shows any of these, don't wait – see a vet ASAP:

  • **Sudden inability to walk** or drags both back legs
  • **Loss of bladder or bowel control** (peeing or pooing without knowing)
  • **Crying out in pain** when touched or moving
  • **Head tilt or circling** (could be a neck or inner ear issue)
  • **Rapid progression** (e.g., was fine last week, now can't jump at all)

The Bottom Line: You Can Make a Huge Difference

Look, none of us want to think about our dogs getting older or developing arthritis. But the truth is, it's incredibly common – especially in our larger breeds. The best thing you can do is be observant, act early, and work with your vet to create a management plan that keeps your best mate comfortable, active, and happy for as long as possible.

Remember: **prevention is better than cure**. Keeping your puppy lean, avoiding over-exercise on hard surfaces while they're growing, and maintaining good posture (yes, even for dogs!) can go a long way to preventing these issues in the first place.
