When Your Dog’s Heart Needs a Little Extra Care

If you’ve got a small, older dog—especially a Cavalier King Charles Spaniel, a Maltese, or a little crossbreed—you might have heard the vet mention a heart murmur. It sounds scary, but many dogs live happy lives with one. However, sometimes that murmur can lead to something more serious: fluid building up in the lungs. That’s called cardiogenic pulmonary edema, and it’s a medical emergency.

But don’t panic. With the right know-how, you can spot trouble early and get your mate the help they need. I’ve just been reading a recent study on exactly this topic, and I want to share the key takeaways in plain Aussie English.

What to Watch For: The Telltale Signs

The first thing you’ll notice is that your dog is breathing faster than normal. The study looked at 100 dogs with this condition, and their average breathing rate when they arrived at the hospital was 64 breaths per minute. For reference, a healthy dog at rest usually breathes between 15 and 30 times per minute.

You might also see your dog working harder to breathe—their tummy heaving, or they’re holding their head out straight. Vets use a score called the Tufts Dyspnea Score (don’t worry about the name) to measure how much trouble they’re having. The higher the score, the more urgent the situation.

What you can do tonight: Get into the habit of counting your dog’s resting breaths when they’re asleep or lying quietly. Set a timer for 30 seconds, count the breaths, then double it. Do this a few times a week. If you see a steady rise above 40 breaths per minute, give your vet a call.

Treatment: What Happens at the Vet Hospital

If your dog does end up in hospital with fluid on the lungs, the first thing they’ll get is oxygen therapy. In the study, dogs needed oxygen for an average of 20 hours before they could breathe comfortably on their own. They also get injections of medication to help the heart pump better and get rid of extra fluid.

Here’s some good news: 95% of dogs survived to go home. That’s a huge number. The key is that the dogs whose breathing improved quickly—within the first 12 hours—had the best chance. So early treatment really does save lives.

A Real-Life Story: Meet Buddy the 12-Year-Old Toy Breed

Let me tell you about a little bloke in the study. He was a 12-year-old, 3.3 kg male dog (so a tiny guy) with a heart murmur. When he arrived, he was breathing at 68 breaths per minute and his dyspnea score was 7 out of 10—pretty rough. They gave him oxygen and some injectable meds. Within 12 hours, his breathing dropped to 40 breaths per minute and his score fell to 3. He was home after 24 hours. Stories like his show that even when things look bad, treatment can work wonders.

What About That Loud Heart Murmur? Busting a Myth

A lot of people think a loud murmur means the end is near. But the study found that the loudness of the murmur didn’t predict whether a dog would die in hospital. What it did predict was that dogs with louder murmurs were more likely to need to come back to hospital with another fluid episode within two months. So a loud murmur is a sign to be extra vigilant, not a death sentence.

Preventing a Crisis at Home

The single best thing you can do is monitor your dog’s resting breathing rate. A normal resting rate is below 30–40 breaths per minute. If it creeps up above 40–50 and stays there, especially if your dog seems uncomfortable, call your vet. Early detection means you can adjust medications before a full-blown emergency.

Also, watch the salt. Avoid giving your dog chips, bacon, cheese, or any processed human food. High-sodium diets make the heart work harder by increasing blood volume. Stick to a vet-recommended low-sodium diet and treats.

What About Supplements?

You might have heard about taurine or fish oil for heart health. The study didn’t test supplements, so there’s no specific advice from this paper. Always talk to your vet before adding anything to your dog’s routine—some supplements can interact with heart medications.

The Bottom Line

Managing a dog with a heart condition is a bit like being a detective. You’re looking for small changes in breathing, energy, and appetite. The research shows that with close monitoring and quick action, most dogs survive an episode and can enjoy quality time with their families. Buddy is a great example—he’s proof that even a tiny old dog can bounce back.

Your homework: Grab a notebook or use your phone’s notes app. This week, start counting your dog’s resting breaths. Write down the number and the date. If you ever see a sudden jump, you’ll have a clear record to show your vet. That simple habit could save your dog’s life.

Stay well, and give your furry mate a scratch from me.
