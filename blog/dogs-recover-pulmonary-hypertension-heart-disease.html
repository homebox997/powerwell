Can Dogs Recover from Pulmonary Hypertension? New Hope for Heart Disease in Aussie Pups

By PawWell Editorial · 2026-07-23 · heart

When Your Dog's Heart Needs a Little Extra Help

If you're reading this, chances are you've noticed your beloved mate isn't quite themselves lately. Maybe they're coughing more than usual, getting tired on walks, or struggling to catch their breath. As a fellow dog owner, I know how scary it can be when our furry family members aren't feeling their best.

Today, we're going to talk about something that sounds pretty intimidating: **pulmonary hypertension** (PH) in dogs. But don't worry – we'll break it down into plain English, and I'll share some encouraging news that might just give you hope.

What Is Pulmonary Hypertension, Really?

Imagine your dog's heart as a pump, and their lungs as a filter. Pulmonary hypertension happens when the blood pressure in the arteries leading from the heart to the lungs gets too high. It's like trying to push water through a garden hose that's been kinked – the heart has to work much harder.

This condition often shows up in dogs who already have **mitral valve disease** (a common heart problem, especially in smaller breeds). In fact, studies show that about **4 out of 10 dogs** with advanced mitral valve disease also develop pulmonary hypertension.

The Hidden Signs You Might Miss

Here's the tricky part: early on, PH can be completely silent. Your dog might seem perfectly fine. But as it progresses, you might notice:

  • **Coughing** – especially at night or after exercise
  • **Breathing fast** or struggling to catch their breath
  • **Getting tired quickly** on walks they used to love
  • **A swollen belly** (from fluid buildup)
  • **Blue-ish gums or tongue** (a serious sign)

If your small breed dog has been diagnosed with a heart murmur or mitral valve disease, and you spot any of these signs, **get them to the vet pronto**.

The Good News: It Can Be Reversed!

Here's the part that made me smile: a common myth is that once a dog develops pulmonary hypertension, it's permanent and untreatable. But new research is turning that idea on its head.

A study from Japan and the University of Florida looked at **21 dogs** with severe mitral valve disease and PH who underwent **mitral valve repair surgery**. The results were impressive:

  • Most dogs showed **significant improvement** in heart function
  • **71.4%** of dogs achieved complete resolution of PH after surgery
  • Their hearts actually **returned to normal size** in many cases

Yes, you read that right – the condition can be reversible! Dr. Tomohiko Yoshida and his team found that fixing the underlying mitral valve problem can take the pressure off the lungs and let the heart heal.

But Surgery Isn't for Every Dog

Now, before you rush off to book surgery, let's be real. Mitral valve repair is a major procedure that requires a specialist veterinary cardiologist and a dedicated team. It's not available everywhere in Australia, and it can be expensive.

For many dogs, **medical management** is still the mainstay of treatment. This includes:

  • Medications to help the heart pump better
  • Drugs to lower blood pressure in the lungs
  • Diuretics to remove excess fluid

What You Can Do Tonight: Practical Tips

Whether your dog is managing with medication or recovering from surgery, here are some things you can do right now to help:

1. Watch the Salt

This is a big one. **High-sodium diets** can make PH worse by causing fluid retention. Check your dog's food label – if salt is high on the ingredient list, consider switching to a low-sodium veterinary diet. Even treats like cheese, bacon, and commercial dog treats can be loaded with salt.

2. Keep Them Lean

Extra weight puts more strain on the heart. If your dog needs to lose a few kilos, talk to your vet about a safe weight loss plan.

3. Monitor Their Breathing

Learn what your dog's normal breathing rate is (count breaths per minute when they're resting). If it suddenly increases, that's a red flag.

4. Know When to Call the Vet

If your dog collapses, has difficulty breathing, or their gums turn blue, **this is an emergency**. Don't wait – get to the nearest vet.

Supplements: Helpful but Not a Cure

You might have heard about supplements like **omega-3 fatty acids, taurine, or L-carnitine** for heart health. While these can support overall heart function, there's **no proven supplement** that treats pulmonary hypertension directly. Always check with your vet before starting any supplement – some can interact with medications.

The Importance of Regular Check-Ups

If your dog has mitral valve disease, especially if they're in **Stage C** (showing signs of heart failure), regular monitoring is crucial. Your vet should check:

  • **Chest X-rays** – to look for enlarged pulmonary arteries
  • **Heart ultrasound** – to measure pressures in the lungs
  • **Blood pressure** – to assess overall heart function

Aim for check-ups every **3 to 6 months**. This way, if PH develops, you can catch it early when treatment is most effective.

A Word About Recurrence

Even after successful surgery, a small number of dogs (about **14%** in the study) can have PH come back months later. This is why ongoing monitoring is so important, even if your dog seems perfectly fine.

The Bottom Line

Pulmonary hypertension sounds scary, but it doesn't have to be a death sentence. With advances in veterinary medicine, many dogs can live happy, comfortable lives – and some can even be cured.

The key is **early detection and proactive management**. If your dog has a heart condition, work closely with your vet, keep up with regular check-ups, and don't ignore those subtle signs.

As always, your pup depends on you to be their advocate. You've got this – and your vet has your back.
