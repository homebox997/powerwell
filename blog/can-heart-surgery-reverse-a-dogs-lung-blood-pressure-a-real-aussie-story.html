When Your Best Mate's Heart Needs a Helping Hand

If your dog has been diagnosed with a heart murmur or early heart disease, you might have heard the scary term "pulmonary hypertension" (PH). It sounds like something from a textbook, but honestly, it just means the blood pressure in the lungs is too high. And for many dogs, especially little ones like Cavaliers and bigger mates like Golden Retrievers, it can make breathing tough and leave them feeling flat.

Here's the good news: a recent study from vets in Japan and the US showed that for some dogs, this high pressure in the lungs can actually be reversed with the right surgery. Let's break it down in plain English.

What Actually Happens?

Picture your dog's heart as a pump. When a valve (the mitral valve) starts leaking, the pump has to work harder. That extra pressure backs up into the lungs, making it hard for your pooch to catch their breath. They might:

  • Cough more, especially at night or after a walk
  • Get tired quickly on a stroll around the block
  • Have fainting spells (syncope, but we just call it collapsing)
  • Breathe heavily even when resting

In the study, 71.4% of dogs had all their symptoms disappear after the surgery. That's a huge win.

The Aussie Hero Story: A Golden Retriever's Comeback

Meet a 9-year-old Golden Retriever who was struggling. Constant coughing, collapsing episodes, and even the best meds weren't helping. His family was worried sick. He had Stage D heart disease (the most severe stage) and severe pulmonary hypertension.

He had mitral valve repair surgery – that's open-heart surgery for dogs, done under a heart-lung machine. Sounds intense, right? But within three months, his heart chambers had returned to normal size, and his lung pressure was back to a safe level. He was running around like a pup again.

Can This Happen to Any Dog?

Not every dog is a candidate for this surgery. It's a big operation, and it's not available everywhere in Australia yet (though specialist centres in Sydney, Melbourne, and Brisbane are doing amazing work). But the key takeaway is: pulmonary hypertension is not always a life sentence.

What You Can Do Tonight (No Surgery Required)

While you're thinking about long-term options, here's practical advice you can act on right now:

1. Check Your Dog's Food Bowl

High-salt diets are a big no-no for dogs with heart issues. Salt makes them retain fluid, which makes the heart work even harder. Look for low-sodium dog foods or ask your vet about a cardiac diet. Avoid giving them chips, processed treats, or anything from your plate that's salty.

2. Listen to Their Breathing

When your dog is sleeping, count how many breaths they take in a minute. Normal is under 30. If it's consistently above 35-40, call your vet. This can be a sign fluid is building up.

3. Know the Warning Signs

If your dog starts coughing after drinking water, or collapses after excitement, don't just put it down to old age. Get an echocardiogram (heart ultrasound) – it's the best way to catch PH early.

4. Supplements? Not a Cure

You'll see products online promising to fix heart issues. While omega-3s, taurine, and L-carnitine can support heart health, they won't reverse pulmonary hypertension. Don't skip your vet's advice for a supplement.

The Bottom Line

If your dog has a heart murmur and you're worried about their breathing, don't lose hope. New treatments like mitral valve repair are changing the game. The earlier you catch it, the better the outcome.

Talk to your vet about a heart ultrasound. And remember – even if surgery isn't an option, managing their diet and watching for symptoms can make a world of difference.
