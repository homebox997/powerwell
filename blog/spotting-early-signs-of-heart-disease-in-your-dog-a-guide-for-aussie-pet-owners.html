Is Your Dog Coughing More Than Usual? It Could Be Their Heart

Hey there, fellow dog lover! If you're reading this while your pup is curled up next to you, you're probably already tuned into their little quirks and habits. But have you noticed a new cough, especially at night or after a walk? It might be easy to brush off as a cold or just 'one of those things', but for our small-breed mates, it could be something more serious: heart disease.

What Is MVD? (It's the Most Common Heart Problem in Dogs)

MVD stands for myxomatous mitral valve disease – but let's just call it a leaky heart valve. It's the number one heart condition in dogs, especially small breeds like Cavaliers, Poodles, Chihuahuas, and even your average Maltese or Shih Tzu. Basically, the valve between two chambers of the heart gets floppy and doesn't close properly. This lets blood leak backwards, making the heart work harder.

Over time, this extra work can lead to a condition called congestive heart failure (CHF). But here's the good news: if you catch it early, you can manage it and give your dog many more happy, comfortable years.

Early Warning Signs Every Owner Should Know

Here's the tricky part – early MVD often has no symptoms. That's why regular vet check-ups are a lifesaver. But once the disease progresses, your dog might start showing these signs:

  • Coughing – especially at night, when lying down, or after exercise. This is NOT a normal 'dog cold'. It's caused by the enlarged heart pressing on the windpipe or fluid building up in the lungs.
  • Faster breathing – even when resting. If your dog's breathing seems rapid or laboured, take note.
  • Restlessness – they might pace, have trouble settling, or seem anxious, especially at night.
  • Tiredness – less enthusiasm for walks or playtime.
  • Blue-ish tongue or gums – this is a sign of poor oxygen and an emergency.

> Real Aussie example: My neighbour's Cavalier, Bella, started coughing after her morning walk. Her owner thought it was just dust. But when Bella couldn't settle at night and kept panting, they rushed to the vet. Lucky they did – she was in early-stage CHF. With medication and a low-salt diet, Bella is now back to chasing balls (just a bit slower).

What To Do If You Suspect Heart Trouble

First, don't panic! Book an appointment with your vet. They'll listen for a heart murmur with a stethoscope – that's often the first clue. If they hear something, they might recommend an X-ray or ultrasound to check the heart size.

If your dog is diagnosed with MVD or CHF, the vet will likely start them on a combination of medications. These aren't scary – think of them as a toolkit to help the heart work easier:

  • Diuretics (like frusemide) – to flush out extra fluid
  • ACE inhibitors – to relax blood vessels
  • Sometimes a mild heart strengthener (like digoxin)

The key is strictly following the vet's instructions – never skip doses, and never change the amount without checking.

Home Management: What You Can Do Tonight

Managing a dog with heart disease at home is totally doable. Here's how:

1. Switch to a Low-Salt Diet

Sodium (salt) is the enemy for a weak heart. It makes the body hold onto water, which overloads the heart. So no more chip crumbs, ham, bacon, or salty treats. Even cheese and bread can be surprisingly high in salt.

What to feed instead:
  • Prescription heart-friendly dog food (ask your vet)
  • Fresh, lean meats (chicken, beef) cooked without salt
  • Low-sodium veggies like green beans or pumpkin

> Aussie tip: Check the labels on your dog's favourite treats. Many Australian-made treats are fine, but some 'natural' chews or jerky can have added salt. When in doubt, ask your vet.

2. Keep an Eye on Their Water

Don't restrict water unless your vet says so. But do measure how much they drink – a sudden increase can be a sign of worsening heart failure.

3. Make Life Easy

  • Avoid strenuous exercise, especially in hot weather
  • Keep them cool in summer (Aussie heat is tough on hearts!)
  • Use a harness instead of a collar to avoid pressure on the throat and neck

4. Watch Their Breathing

Learn to count their resting respiratory rate. When your dog is sleeping, count how many breaths they take in 15 seconds and multiply by four. Normal is under 30 breaths per minute. If it's consistently higher, call your vet.

Supplements: Helpful but Only With Vet Guidance

Some supplements might support heart health, but never add them without talking to your vet first. Here's what's sometimes used:

  • Omega-3 fatty acids (fish oil) – can reduce inflammation
  • Taurine – an amino acid that some dogs need more of
  • Coenzyme Q10 – helps with energy in heart cells

But remember: too much fish oil can cause bleeding problems, and taurine is only useful if your dog is deficient. Always get the green light from your vet.

Common Myth: 'It's Just a Cough'

Many owners mistake a heart cough for a cold or kennel cough. Here's the difference:

| Heart Cough | Cold/Kennel Cough |

|-------------|-------------------|

| Worse at night or lying down | Comes with sneezing, runny nose |

| Dog seems tired, restless | Dog still has energy |

| Tongue might look blue | Normal gum colour |

| Doesn't respond to cough medicine | Might improve with rest |

If your small-breed dog (especially over 7 years old) has a persistent cough, don't guess – get them checked.

The Big Picture: You and Your Vet Are a Team

Heart disease in dogs is a long-term condition, not a one-off fix. But with today's treatments, many dogs live happily for years after diagnosis. The most important things are:

  • Regular vet visits (every 6–12 months)
  • Sticking to medication (even when they seem fine)
  • Watching for changes (cough, breathing, energy)
  • Loving them just the same

Your vet is your best mate in this journey. Don't be afraid to ask questions, and never feel silly for calling if you're worried. That's what we're all here for – helping you help your furry family.
