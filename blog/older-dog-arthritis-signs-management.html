Older Dog Struggling to Get Up? How to Spot and Manage Canine Arthritis at Home

By PawWell Editorial · 2026-07-23 · joint

Is Your Dog Slowing Down? It Might Be Arthritis

You know that feeling when you wake up and your knees creak like an old gate? Well, our furry mates can feel it too. Canine osteoarthritis (or just "arthritis" for short) is incredibly common, especially in our older dogs. It's a chronic, progressive condition where the cushioning cartilage in the joints wears down, causing pain, stiffness, and reduced mobility.

But here’s the good news: with early detection and the right care, you can make a world of difference to your dog’s quality of life. Think of this as your friendly guide to spotting the signs and taking action tonight.

Spotting the Early Signs (Before It Gets Bad)

Sometimes we put our dog's stiffness down to "just getting old." But it doesn’t have to be that way. Here are the tell-tale signs to watch for:

  • **Morning stiffness:** Your dog might take a few minutes to get going after a nap or a night's sleep. They might limp for the first 5–15 minutes of a walk, then loosen up.
  • **Reluctance to move:** They might hesitate at the bottom of the stairs, avoid jumping onto the couch, or be slower to get in and out of the car.
  • **Changes in gait:** You might notice a subtle limp, or they may start "bunny hopping" with their back legs when running.
  • **Behaviour changes:** A normally friendly dog might snap if you touch a sore joint. They might also seem more tired or less interested in play.
  • **Weather sensitivity:** Some dogs seem stiffer on cold, damp days or before a storm.

**Real-life example:** Take Bella, a 10-year-old Golden Retriever. Her owner noticed she was stiff for about 15 minutes every morning, and she stopped jumping onto her favourite spot on the sofa. A vet visit and X-rays confirmed arthritis in both knees. With a weight loss plan, joint supplements, and gentle daily walks, her morning stiffness dropped to just 5 minutes within a month.

What Actually Causes Arthritis in Dogs?

It's not just old age. Several factors can speed up joint wear and tear:

  • **Excess weight:** This is the big one. Every extra kilogram puts massive pressure on joints. Overweight dogs are *twice* as likely to develop arthritis.
  • **Previous injuries:** A past sprain, fracture, or cruciate ligament tear can set the stage for arthritis later.
  • **Genetics:** Some breeds, especially Labs, Goldens, and German Shepherds, are more prone to joint issues.
  • **Certain medications:** Surprising but true – long-term use of some antibiotics (like enrofloxacin) or steroids (like prednisone) can weaken ligaments and increase the risk of cruciate disease.

What You Can Do Tonight (Actionable Tips)

Here’s the practical part. You don’t need to wait for a vet appointment to start helping your dog feel better.

1. Check Their Weight

Grab a kitchen scale or use a vet’s scale. If your dog is overweight, even losing 5–10% of their body weight can dramatically reduce joint pain. For a 30kg Labrador, that’s just 1.5–3kg.

**Tonight’s tip:** Measure your dog’s food with a proper cup (not a scoop) and cut out extra treats. Swap biscuits for green beans or carrot sticks as low-calorie rewards.

2. Make Small Home Adjustments

  • **Provide soft bedding:** A thick orthopaedic bed can cushion sore joints. Avoid cold, hard floors.
  • **Use ramps or steps:** If your dog struggles with stairs or jumping onto furniture, a pet ramp can save their joints.
  • **Non-slip mats:** Put rugs or yoga mats on slippery tiles to give your dog better grip.

3. Gentle Exercise (Not Rest!)

Rest is not the answer. Controlled, low-impact movement helps keep joints lubricated and muscles strong.

  • **Short, frequent walks:** Instead of one long walk, try two or three shorter ones (10–15 minutes each).
  • **Swimming or hydrotherapy:** If you have access, swimming is fantastic – it’s zero-impact but builds muscle.
  • **Avoid:** Running, jumping, fetch on hard surfaces, and stairs.

4. Joint Supplements (The Right Way)

Many joint supplements can help, but they need the right dose and time to work (usually 4–8 weeks).

For a 20kg dog, aim for:

  • **Glucosamine:** 300–400 mg per day (15–20 mg per kg)
  • **Chondroitin:** 200–300 mg per day (10–15 mg per kg)
  • **Omega-3 (fish oil):** 600–1000 mg EPA/DHA per day (30–50 mg per kg)

**Important:** Always check with your vet before starting supplements, especially if your dog is on other medications.

When to See the Vet

If your dog is showing any of the signs above, it’s worth a vet check. They can do a physical exam and often recommend X-rays to confirm arthritis. Early diagnosis means better management.

**Warning:** Never give your dog human painkillers like ibuprofen or paracetamol. They are toxic to dogs and can cause kidney failure or stomach ulcers. Always use vet-prescribed medications.

The Big Picture: It’s About Quality of Life

Arthritis can’t be cured, but it can be managed. With the right combination of weight control, gentle exercise, joint supplements, and sometimes vet-prescribed anti-inflammatories (like NSAIDs), most dogs can live comfortably for years.

And remember – if your dog is in severe pain and nothing else works, joint replacement surgery is available for dogs and can give them a new lease on life.

Final Thoughts

Your older dog still has plenty of love and tail wags left. By paying attention to the early signs and making a few simple changes, you can help them stay mobile, happy, and by your side for as long as possible.
