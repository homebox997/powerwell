Your Dog's Heart Health: What Every Aussie Pet Owner Needs to Know About Mitral Valve Disease

By PawWell Editorial · 2026-07-23 · heart

Introduction: A Heart-to-Heart About Your Dog's Ticker

G'day, fellow dog lovers! If you're like me, your furry mate is part of the family. You notice the little things—the way they wag their tail when you come home, that extra cuddle on a rainy day. But what about the things you can't see? Like what's going on inside their heart?

Today, we're talking about something that affects a surprising number of our small breed dogs: myxomatous mitral valve disease (or MMVD for short). Don't worry, I'll keep the jargon to a minimum. Think of it as a chat over a cuppa at the dog park.

What Is This Heart Thing, Anyway?

Imagine your dog's heart as a four-room house. The mitral valve is like a door between two rooms on the left side. In MMVD, that door gets a bit floppy over time—like an old screen door that doesn't quite shut properly. This means a little blood can leak backwards, which makes the heart work harder.

It's incredibly common. Studies show that between 30% and 70% of dogs over 10 years old have some form of this disease. That's a lot of senior pups! Small breeds are especially prone, particularly **Cavalier King Charles Spaniels, Dachshunds, and Miniature Poodles**.

The Sneaky Part: No Symptoms at First

Here's the tricky bit: in the early stages (called Stage B1 and B2), your dog might seem absolutely fine. No coughing, no slowing down, no trouble breathing. That's why it's called "preclinical"—the disease is there, but your pooch is compensating like a champ.

  • **Stage B1:** The heart is starting to change, but not enough to need medication.
  • **Stage B2:** The left side of the heart has enlarged significantly, and treatment is usually recommended.

Take Charlie, a 9-year-old Cavalier King Charles Spaniel from Melbourne. He was diagnosed during a routine checkup—his owner had no idea anything was wrong. An ultrasound showed his heart chambers were enlarged, putting him in Stage B2. But here's the good news: Charlie started on medication and continues to live a happy, active life.

When Does Treatment Start?

A common question I hear is, "When should my dog start heart meds?" The answer is clear: not until Stage B2. For Stage B1 dogs, vets generally recommend monitoring with regular checkups, but no drugs are needed. Starting too early can actually cause more harm than good.

Once your dog reaches Stage B2, your vet might prescribe:

  • **Pimobendan** (helps the heart pump more efficiently)
  • **ACE inhibitors** (relax blood vessels)
  • **Diuretics** (reduce fluid buildup)

Remember, these are only needed when the heart has significantly enlarged. And many dogs in Stage B2 stay stable for years with proper care.

Busting a Myth: Stage B2 Doesn't Mean Dangerous Heart Rhythms

I've heard some owners worry that once their dog's heart enlarges, dangerous arrhythmias (irregular heartbeats) are inevitable. That's a myth! A recent study comparing Stage B1 and B2 dogs found no significant increase in arrhythmia risk in Stage B2. While some ECG measurements change, the body's natural balance between the sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems (your dog's "fight or flight" vs. "rest and digest" systems) remains stable. So take a deep breath—Stage B2 is not a ticking time bomb.

How to Catch It Early

Because MMVD shows no early symptoms, routine vet checkups are your best friend. Your vet will listen for a heart murmur with a stethoscope (auscultation). If they hear something, they'll likely recommend an echocardiogram (heart ultrasound) and an electrocardiogram (ECG) to see what's going on.

For small breed dogs over 8 years old, I recommend annual heart screenings—just like you'd get your own blood pressure checked.

What You Can Do at Home: Supporting Your Dog's Heart

1. Diet Matters: The Sodium Balancing Act

You might think a low-salt diet is best for heart health, but hold your horses! Severe sodium restriction can actually backfire in early stages. It can activate a system in the body (the RAAS system) that worsens fluid retention and heart remodeling.

Instead, aim for a **balanced diet with moderate sodium levels**. Avoid super salty treats like cheese or processed meats, but don't go overboard with "heart diets" unless your vet specifically recommends them for advanced disease.

2. Omega-3 Fatty Acids: A Natural Boost

Omega-3s (EPA and DHA) are like a soothing balm for the heart. They have anti-inflammatory properties and can help reduce oxidative stress. The typical dose is **40–100 mg per kg of body weight per day** of combined EPA and DHA, given with food for better absorption.

You can find these in fish oil supplements made for dogs. Always check with your vet before starting any supplement.

3. Keep an Eye on Breathing

If your dog does progress to heart failure, one of the first signs is **increased breathing rate**. In a study of dogs with fluid in the lungs (pulmonary edema), the average respiratory rate was 64 breaths per minute when they arrived at the vet. Normal is around 15–30 breaths per minute at rest.

**Actionable tip:** Learn your dog's normal resting breathing rate. When they're asleep, count how many times their chest rises in 15 seconds, then multiply by 4. If it's consistently above 30, or if you see labored breathing, contact your vet.

Real-Life Story: Charlie's Journey

Charlie's owner, Sarah from Sydney, was worried when he was diagnosed with Stage B2 MMVD. "I thought he was going to drop dead any minute," she told me. But her vet explained that many dogs live years with this condition. Charlie started on pimobendan and has regular checkups. He still loves his walks, plays fetch, and snores on the couch. The key was early detection and consistent care.

The Bottom Line

MMVD is common, but it's not a death sentence. With regular vet checkups, a balanced diet, and maybe some omega-3s, your small breed dog can enjoy a good quality of life for years. Listen to your vet, trust your instincts, and give your mate an extra belly rub today.
